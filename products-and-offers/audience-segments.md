# Audience Segments — Who Buys What and Why

Three tiers of audience, each with different motivations, different willingness to pay, and different conversion paths. Every product in this workspace maps to one of these segments.

---

## Tier 1: The Curious Learner

**Largest audience, lowest per-person revenue.**

### Who They Are

New developers, vibe coders, career changers, tech-curious professionals, students. They're early in their understanding of AI and computing fundamentals. They know AI matters but don't yet have a mental framework for thinking about it clearly. Many of them are consuming content from dozens of creators and trying to piece together a coherent picture.

### What They Want

Understanding. Mental models. The feeling of "now I get it." They're not looking for certifications or credentials — they want clarity. They want to walk away from a video or a product feeling like they can explain something they couldn't explain before. They want to stop feeling lost in conversations about AI.

### What They'll Pay For

Digital products in the $19-99 range. Things they can buy once, use immediately, and reference repeatedly:

- Prompt libraries with reasoning behind each prompt
- Code templates they can learn from and modify
- Architectural cheat sheets and decision frameworks
- The "Computational Orchestration Toolkit"

The price point matters here. Low enough to be an impulse buy, high enough to feel like a real product. These buyers are spending their own money, not a company budget.

### How Content Converts Them

Top-of-funnel videos (shorts, hooks, "did you know" content) make them feel smart. They share it. They come back. Middle-of-funnel videos (deeper explanations, frameworks) make them want resources — something they can hold onto after the video ends. A well-placed "I have a toolkit that covers all of this" converts. The transition from free to paid feels natural because the paid product is a tangible extension of the free content, not a gate in front of it.

---

## Tier 2: The Builder

**Medium audience, medium revenue.**

### Who They Are

Developers actively integrating AI into products. Technical leads making architecture decisions. Smart business operators building AI-adjacent tools or workflows. They're past the "what is AI" phase and deep into the "how do I build with AI correctly" phase. They've probably already shipped something with AI in it and hit the limitations of their first approach.

### What They Want

Methods that work. Frameworks they can implement Monday morning. Architecture guidance that saves them from expensive mistakes. They don't want theory for theory's sake — they want theory that translates directly into better systems. They're evaluating whether to invest time and money in deeper learning, and they need to see evidence that the teaching is practical and battle-tested.

### What They'll Pay For

Courses, cohorts, and workshops in the $200-1,000 range:

- Self-paced courses on AI fundamentals, prompt architecture, system design
- Cohort-based programs with feedback loops and peer learning
- Intensive workshops on specific technical challenges

Many of these buyers can expense the cost or justify it as professional development. The price point reflects the depth and the outcome — not just understanding, but capability.

### How Content Converts Them

Middle-of-funnel videos demonstrate teaching quality. They watch a 10-minute breakdown of system design principles and think "this person actually knows what they're talking about." They watch enough to realize the free content is a compressed version of something much deeper. They want the full version — the uncompressed curriculum with exercises, examples, and feedback. The conversion moment is when they realize that figuring it out on their own will take longer and cost more (in mistakes) than buying the course.

---

## Tier 3: The Decision-Maker

**Smallest audience, highest revenue.**

### Who They Are

CTOs, VPs of Engineering, IT Directors, government program managers, consultants evaluating AI strategy for their organizations. They're not building with AI themselves — they're deciding whether and how their organizations should. They have budget authority. They have career risk. A bad AI adoption initiative reflects directly on them.

### What They Want

Someone who can help their organization avoid expensive mistakes. Confidence that AI adoption won't be another failed initiative that wastes six months and a million dollars. They want a partner who understands both the technical reality and the organizational complexity — someone who won't just say "use AI for everything" but can honestly say "here's where AI fits and here's where it doesn't."

### What They'll Pay For

Consulting, workshops, and training programs in the $5,000-50,000+ range:

- Enterprise AI training (Correlation One/KPMG-style engagements with proven 95% adoption methods)
- Strategic AI audits evaluating where AI fits vs. where traditional solutions work better
- Executive workshop series on AI strategy and implementation
- Ongoing advisory relationships

This is company money, not personal money. The buying process is longer, involves multiple stakeholders, and requires trust built over time.

### How Content Converts Them

They see content (often on LinkedIn), recognize deep understanding of both the technical and organizational sides of AI, and reach out. Or — more commonly — they're referred by someone on their team who watches the YouTube content. A developer watches 30 shorts, mentions Jake in a meeting, and the CTO looks him up. The content is the world's longest business card. It doesn't sell directly — it establishes credibility so thoroughly that by the time the sales conversation happens, the hardest part of selling is already done.

---

## Segment Interactions

These tiers aren't isolated. They feed each other:

- A **Curious Learner** who buys a $29 prompt library might become a **Builder** who enrolls in a $497 course a year later.
- A **Builder** who takes a course might recommend Jake to their CTO, generating a **Decision-Maker** lead.
- **Decision-Maker** engagements generate case studies and insights that fuel content for all tiers.

The content system serves all three simultaneously. A single video about prompt architecture might convert a Curious Learner to buy the prompt library, convince a Builder to enroll in the course, and make a Decision-Maker think "this person understands what we need."
