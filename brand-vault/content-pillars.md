# Content Pillars

Eduba's content is organized around five pillars. Each pillar serves a specific strategic purpose, attracts a specific segment of the audience, and sits at a specific position in the marketing funnel. Together, the five pillars cover the full range of Jake's expertise and the full journey from casual viewer to paying customer.

The pillars are not rigid categories. Individual pieces of content can touch multiple pillars. But every piece of content should have a primary pillar — the main thing it is doing — and that primary pillar determines its structure, audience assumptions, and strategic purpose.

---

## Pillar 1: "The Layers Beneath"

### What It Is

Demystifying how technology actually works by revealing the abstraction layers that most people never see. This is the pillar where Jake takes something the audience thinks is simple (printing "Hello World," sending a text message, running a Google search, asking ChatGPT a question) and shows them the extraordinary complexity hidden beneath the surface.

This is a proven winner. The Hello World video concept lives here. The core mechanic is the reveal: you thought this was one thing, but actually, here are the seven layers that make it work.

### Why It Works

This pillar achieves something rare in content: it serves two audiences simultaneously.

**For beginners and non-technical audiences:** It satisfies curiosity. Everyone has wondered what actually happens when they press "send" or when they ask an AI a question. These videos answer that question with clarity and visual beauty. The viewer walks away feeling smarter and less intimidated by technology. That emotional payoff — going from "I don't understand this" to "oh, that's how it works" — is deeply shareable.

**For technical audiences (developers, engineers, IT professionals):** It earns respect. Technical people know how complex these systems are. When they see someone explain that complexity accurately, clearly, and in a way that does not condescend or oversimplify, they respect it. Technical viewers become evangelists because they finally have content they can share with their non-technical colleagues, managers, and family members. "Finally, someone explained this correctly" is a powerful recommendation.

Serving both audiences simultaneously is strategically powerful because it means the same video can go viral in general audiences (broad reach) while building credibility with the technical community (deep trust). Most content creators have to choose between accessible and authoritative. This pillar achieves both.

### Example Topics

- **What happens when you hit "send" on a ChatGPT prompt.** From the keystroke on your keyboard, through your operating system, through your browser, through HTTP/HTTPS, through the internet's physical infrastructure, to OpenAI's servers, through the tokenizer, through the transformer architecture, through the inference process, back through all the same layers, to pixels on your screen. Every step visualized.

- **The 7 layers between Python code and electrons.** The Hello World concept. Print("Hello World") looks like one instruction. It is actually a cascade through the Python interpreter, bytecode compilation, the C runtime, operating system calls, hardware abstraction layers, machine code, and physical transistor state changes. 12,000+ lines of code execute to print two words.

- **How a database query works versus AI inference, and why one costs 1000x more.** A SQL SELECT statement follows a deterministic path through a query parser, optimizer, and storage engine. A ChatGPT query follows a probabilistic path through tokenization, embedding, attention mechanisms, and generation loops. The difference in computational cost is not incremental; it is orders of magnitude. Visualizing this explains why the 60/30/10 framework exists.

- **What "the cloud" actually is, physically.** Not an abstraction in the sky. Physical buildings full of servers in specific locations with specific power requirements, cooling systems, network connections, and failure modes. When you "save to the cloud," here is the physical journey your data takes.

- **How encryption works.** Jake's Marine background in cryptographic systems makes this deeply authentic. Not a generic explainer but a "here's what I worked with" grounded explanation of how encrypted communications actually function, from key exchange to cipher operations to the physical security of the systems involved.

- **What happens inside a neural network, explained through layers people already understand.** Using the abstraction layer framework to explain neural networks by analogy to things the audience already knows: spreadsheet functions (weights and biases as cell formulas), postal sorting (routing and classification), recipe adjustments (backpropagation as iterative refinement).

### Funnel Position

**Top of funnel.** These videos have the broadest appeal because curiosity about how things work is nearly universal. They are optimized for reach, shares, and bringing new people into the Eduba audience. A viewer might have zero interest in AI consulting or courses, but they want to know what happens when they press send. That curiosity gets them in the door. Once inside, they discover the other pillars.

## Pillar 2: "The Right Tool for the Job" (Computational Orchestration)

### What It Is

Teaching the 60/30/10 framework and the broader principle of computational orchestration. The core message: most problems do not need AI. They need the right combination of traditional code, databases, rule-based logic, and AI applied to the specific slice where it actually adds value.

This pillar is where Jake's counter-positioning is most explicitly stated. It is the pillar that says: before you reach for AI, ask yourself whether a SQL query, a Python script, a decision tree, or a human would solve this problem faster, cheaper, and more reliably. The answer is "yes" about 90% of the time.

### Why It Works

This is the most commercially valuable pillar. It directly maps to what organizations pay Jake to teach. When Jake delivers workshops at Pacific Life, Colgate-Palmolive, or KPMG UK, the core curriculum is computational orchestration: how to decompose a business problem into its components and assign each component to the right type of computation.

Every video in this pillar is a soft pitch for consulting without ever making a pitch. A decision-maker who watches a video about why 60% of what their team uses AI for should be a database query will think: "we should bring this guy in to help us figure out our AI strategy." The video taught them something valuable for free and simultaneously demonstrated that Jake has the expertise to help them at an organizational level.

The 60/30/10 framework is the centerpiece. It is memorable (three numbers), counterintuitive (most people assume AI should handle more than 10%), and immediately actionable (go audit your current AI usage and see how much of it should be handled by simpler tools). Frameworks that are memorable, counterintuitive, and actionable spread through organizations. People bring them to meetings, cite them in strategy documents, and share them with colleagues. Each share is implicit brand evangelism.

### Example Topics

- **Why 60% of what people use AI for should be a database query.** Concrete examples of tasks that organizations are using ChatGPT or similar tools for that would be better served by a SQL query against their existing database. Faster, cheaper, more accurate, more reliable. The AI is not wrong; it is just unnecessary.

- **The cost of AI inference versus a SQL query, actual numbers, visualized.** Take a specific, representative task. Show the computational resources (and therefore cost) of handling it with AI inference versus a database query. The difference is typically 100x to 1000x. Visualize that difference so it is viscerally clear, not just intellectually understood.

- **When AI is the wrong answer: real examples.** Case studies from Jake's consulting work (anonymized as needed) where organizations were using AI for tasks that were better handled by traditional approaches. What the AI implementation cost, what the simpler alternative cost, and what the outcome difference was.

- **The "AI tax": automating something that did not need automating.** The concept of the AI tax: the additional cost, complexity, and failure modes you introduce when you use AI for a task that had a perfectly good non-AI solution. Sometimes the AI tax is worth it. Most of the time, it is not.

- **How to decompose a business problem into AI, code, and human components.** A practical framework for taking a business problem and breaking it into pieces, then assigning each piece to the right type of computation. This is the workshop methodology in video form.

- **Why the 95% adoption rate matters.** Using Jake's own results to illustrate a broader point: when you teach people the right tool for the job instead of "use AI for everything," adoption rates skyrocket because the training matches reality. People adopt what actually works.

### Funnel Position

**Middle of funnel.** These videos attract decision-makers: managers, directors, VPs, and C-suite executives who are responsible for their organization's AI strategy. These people are not browsing for entertainment. They are looking for frameworks they can apply. When they find Jake's content, they are one step from becoming a consulting lead. This pillar builds the consulting pipeline directly.

## Pillar 3: "Methods, Not Tools"

### What It Is

AI and software concepts that work across every tool, model, and platform. Not "how to use Claude" but "how to think about prompt architecture." Not "the best ChatGPT plugins" but "how to evaluate any AI tool for your use case." Not "Midjourney vs DALL-E" but "how image generation models work and what matters for quality."

This pillar teaches transferable frameworks rather than tool-specific tutorials. The content is designed to remain valuable regardless of which AI tools exist six months from now.

### Why It Works

Tool-specific content has a shelf life measured in months. When GPT-5 launches, every GPT-4 tutorial becomes partially obsolete. When a new AI tool emerges, every comparison video needs updating. Creators in the tool-tutorial lane are on a treadmill, forever chasing updates. Their content is disposable by design.

Method-based content compounds. Someone who learns prompt architecture — how to structure inputs to any language model for consistent, high-quality outputs — comes back when the next model drops, because the methods still apply. The specific syntax might change, but the structural principles endure.

This creates lasting audience loyalty. A viewer who learned methods from Jake does not need to find a new creator every time a new tool launches. They come back to Jake because Jake teaches the layer beneath the tool, the layer that transfers. This is the abstraction layer philosophy applied to content strategy: Jake teaches at the layer that persists, not the layer that changes.

Methods content also positions Jake as genuinely knowledgeable rather than just current. Anyone can make a tutorial by following the documentation. Teaching transferable methods requires actual understanding of why things work, not just how to use them. This filters the audience toward people who value depth, which is exactly the audience that converts to customers.

### Example Topics

- **Prompt architecture: structure matters more than wording.** Most "prompt engineering" content focuses on magic words and secret phrases. The actual skill is structural: how you organize context, instructions, constraints, and examples in a prompt. That structure works across Claude, GPT, Gemini, Llama, and whatever comes next.

- **The mental model: probability engine, not a brain.** The single most important conceptual shift for anyone using AI: it is not thinking. It is predicting the most likely next token. Understanding this changes how you write prompts, how you evaluate outputs, and how you decide when to use AI versus something else.

- **How to evaluate AI output quality.** A framework for assessing whether AI output is good enough for a given use case. Not "is it correct?" (too binary) but "does it meet the quality bar for this specific application, and what are the failure modes I need to watch for?"

- **System design patterns across Claude, GPT, Gemini, and local models.** The architectural patterns that work regardless of which model you are using: chain-of-thought prompting, retrieval-augmented generation, output validation loops, human-in-the-loop checkpoints. The pattern is the skill; the model is the implementation detail.

- **The difference between "using AI" and "building with AI."** Using AI is interacting with a chat interface. Building with AI is integrating AI into a system with inputs, outputs, error handling, and evaluation metrics. Most people think they want to "use AI" when they actually need to "build with AI," and the skill sets are different.

- **How to read documentation and adapt.** A meta-skill that most content creators never teach: how to read technical documentation, extract what matters for your use case, and adapt examples to your specific situation. This is the skill that makes all other skills transferable.

### Funnel Position

**Middle of funnel.** These videos convert watchers into people who want resources and training. Someone who watches a methods video and has an "aha" moment about prompt architecture is primed to want more structured learning. They are the natural audience for courses, workshops, and downloadable resources. This pillar is the bridge between casual viewing and active learning.

## Pillar 4: "The Builder's Architecture"

### What It Is

Software fundamentals, system design, and problem-solving frameworks presented in a way that is accessible to non-engineers but respected by engineers. The core message: coding and building are about structure and strategy, not just syntax. You do not need to memorize every function. You need to understand how systems fit together.

This pillar is specifically designed for the era of AI-assisted coding, where people are generating code with AI tools but often do not understand the architectural principles that determine whether that code will work at scale.

### Why It Works

The "vibe coding" generation needs this pillar urgently. A new category of builders has emerged: people who use AI to generate code, ship functional prototypes, and build real applications without formal engineering training. Many of them are producing impressive results. But they are also producing applications that work until they do not, because they lack understanding of architecture, design patterns, scalability, and the principles that separate a prototype from a production system.

Jake bridges that gap. He does not gatekeep ("you need a CS degree to build software"). He does not naively celebrate ("anyone can be an engineer now"). He does something more useful: he teaches the structural principles that make the difference between code that works and code that works reliably, at scale, under real-world conditions. And he teaches these principles in a way that people who came to coding through AI can actually absorb, because he uses physical metaphors, visual explanations, and the same abstraction layer framework that underpins all of Eduba's content.

Engineers respect this content because it is technically accurate and grounded in real architectural principles. Non-engineers appreciate it because it makes them genuinely better builders, not just better prompters. This dual audience effect is the same dynamic that makes Pillar 1 work, applied to a different domain.

### Example Topics

- **Why AI-generated code works until it does not.** The specific failure modes of AI-generated code: it often solves the immediate problem without considering edge cases, error handling, scalability, or integration with existing systems. Not a criticism of AI coding tools, but a clear-eyed explanation of where human architectural understanding is still essential.

- **Design patterns through physical metaphors.** The factory pattern explained through an actual factory. The observer pattern explained through a newspaper subscription. The singleton pattern explained through a government office. Making abstract software concepts tangible through physical analogies that people can remember and apply.

- **How to think about a project before writing a line of code.** The planning and architecture phase that most vibe coders skip. What questions to ask, what decisions to make, what constraints to identify, before any code (human-written or AI-generated) is produced. This is where the real engineering skill lives.

- **The abstraction principle applied to business problems.** Taking the core concept of abstraction layers and applying it to business process design, organizational structure, and project management. Showing that the same principle that makes software manageable makes business problems manageable.

- **What "technical debt" means, even with AI-written code.** Technical debt does not disappear because AI wrote the code. In fact, AI-generated code can accumulate technical debt faster because it often favors short-term functionality over long-term maintainability. Understanding technical debt is essential for anyone building with AI assistance.

- **APIs: how software talks to software.** A foundational explainer that is surprisingly underserved in the content landscape. What APIs are, how they work, why they matter, and how they fit into the larger system architecture. Essential knowledge for anyone building AI-integrated applications.

### Funnel Position

**Middle to bottom of funnel.** This pillar attracts developers and technical business people who are the most likely to become course and resource buyers. They have a specific pain point (they are building things with AI but hitting walls), and Jake's content directly addresses that pain point with actionable knowledge. This audience converts at a high rate because the value proposition is clear and immediate: learn this and your projects will stop breaking.

## Pillar 5: "The Honest Take"

### What It Is

Counter-narrative content that calls out hype, bad practices, and misconceptions in the AI space. This is not cynical or doom-oriented content. It is not "AI is bad" or "AI companies are evil." It is simply honest assessment of what AI can and cannot do, delivered by someone with the credentials and experience to know the difference.

The tone is important: this is a knowledgeable friend pulling you aside and saying "hey, before you buy that, let me tell you what they're not mentioning in the marketing." It is protective, not combative. Caring, not cynical.

### Why It Works

Trust is the primary conversion mechanism for Eduba's business. Not hype, not FOMO, not persuasion. Trust. And trust is built by being honest when honesty is not in your commercial interest.

Every honest take deposits trust in the audience's account. When Jake says "this popular AI tool is actually not as good as the vendor claims, and here's why, with specific evidence," the audience learns three things: (1) a useful fact about the tool, (2) that Jake is not in the pocket of AI vendors, and (3) that Jake prioritizes accuracy over popularity.

That third lesson is the one that converts. When Jake eventually offers something for sale, whether it is a course, a consulting engagement, or a resource, the audience has already decided he is trustworthy. They have watched him debunk, correct, and call out things that a less honest creator would have promoted for affiliate revenue or industry relationships. The sales conversation happens in a context of earned trust, and that changes the conversion dynamic entirely.

The honest take pillar also generates high engagement because it is inherently interesting. "Why most AI demos are misleading" is a topic that everyone who has been impressed by an AI demo wants to understand. "What AI companies don't want you to know about benchmarks" is catnip for the technically curious. Honesty is interesting. It cuts through noise. It gives people language and frameworks to evaluate what they are being told by other sources.

### Example Topics

- **"AI will replace programmers": what is actually happening.** Not "yes it will" or "no it won't" but a nuanced, evidence-based assessment of how AI is changing programming work. What tasks are being automated, what tasks are becoming more important, and what the actual trajectory looks like for different types of development work.

- **Why most AI demos are misleading: the cherry-pick problem.** Every AI demo shows the best output from the best prompt on the most favorable task. In production, you get the average output from average prompts on messy real-world tasks. The gap between demo performance and production performance is the most important thing the AI industry is not talking about.

- **The real cost of building an AI product versus vendor quotes.** What vendors quote for AI implementation versus what it actually costs when you account for data preparation, integration, testing, iteration, maintenance, and the inevitable pivot when the first approach does not work. Actual numbers where possible.

- **Why "prompt engineering" is mostly marketing.** A provocative but defensible position: most of what is sold as "prompt engineering" is basic communication skills dressed up in technical language. The real skill (see Pillar 3) is prompt architecture, system design, and knowing when not to prompt at all. The "prompt engineering" label was invented to sell courses, not to describe a genuine engineering discipline.

- **What AI companies do not want you to know about benchmarks.** How AI benchmarks are designed, how they can be gamed, and why benchmark performance does not reliably predict real-world performance. This is something Jake can speak to with authority given his academic research background.

- **AI that works in demo versus AI in production.** The specific failure modes that emerge when AI moves from a controlled demo environment to production: edge cases, adversarial inputs, data drift, integration failures, and the "long tail" of unusual inputs that demos never cover.

### Funnel Position

**Top of funnel for broad appeal, but also a deep trust-builder for bottom of funnel conversion.** The honest take topics attract wide audiences because everyone wants to know the truth about AI hype. But they also serve the most important role in the conversion journey: they build the trust that makes someone comfortable paying Jake for consulting, courses, or resources. A person who has watched Jake debunk AI snake oil is significantly more likely to trust that Jake's own offerings are legitimate.

## How the Pillars Work Together

The five pillars are not isolated. They form a system.

**Pillar 1 (The Layers Beneath)** brings people in the door. Broad curiosity, high shareability, wide reach. A viewer discovers Jake because they want to know what happens when they type print("Hello World").

**Pillar 5 (The Honest Take)** builds trust early. The new viewer sees Jake calling out AI hype and thinks "this person is honest." Trust is deposited.

**Pillar 3 (Methods, Not Tools)** converts the viewer into a learner. They start watching method-based content because they want transferable skills. They begin to see Jake as a teacher, not just an entertainer.

**Pillar 2 (The Right Tool for the Job)** attracts decision-makers. Managers and executives find the 60/30/10 framework and see its immediate applicability to their organization. They begin to think of Jake as a potential consultant or workshop leader.

**Pillar 4 (The Builder's Architecture)** captures the builders. Developers and technical creators who are building with AI find the architecture content and recognize that Jake understands their problems at a deep level. They become resource and course buyers.

The journey from Pillar 1 to Pillar 4 is not linear for every viewer. Some enter at Pillar 2 because they are decision-makers looking for frameworks. Some enter at Pillar 5 because they are skeptics looking for honesty. But the system is designed so that wherever someone enters, there is a natural path toward deeper engagement and, eventually, toward the products and services that sustain the business.

The content mix across pillars should roughly follow the funnel logic:
- **More volume** in Pillars 1 and 5 (top of funnel, broad appeal, high reach)
- **Consistent presence** in Pillars 2 and 3 (middle of funnel, converting viewers to engaged learners and potential customers)
- **Strategic presence** in Pillar 4 (bottom of funnel, serving the most engaged and highest-converting audience segment)

This is not a rigid formula. The exact mix depends on content calendar priorities, seasonal topics, and business goals. But the principle holds: top of funnel content should be produced more frequently than bottom of funnel content, because the top of the funnel needs to stay full.
