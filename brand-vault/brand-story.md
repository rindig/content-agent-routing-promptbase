# Brand Story

## The Story People Will Tell About You

People do not share facts. They share stories. The most important strategic question for any personal brand is not "what do I want to say about myself?" It is "what story do I want other people to tell about me when I am not in the room?"

Here is the story that the Eduba audience should eventually be able to tell others:

> "There's this guy who was a Marine working on cryptographic systems and fighter jet avionics. Like, if his code was wrong, people died. Then he got a master's in AI governance from Edinburgh, published actual research on AI bias, and now he teaches Fortune 500 companies about AI. But here's the thing: his whole approach is about when NOT to use AI. He makes these incredible animations that show you how technology actually works, layer by layer. He's basically the 3Blue1Brown of AI for business. And he's not trying to sell you anything, he's just explaining how things work."

This story is engineered, but it does not feel engineered. It feels like something someone would naturally say after watching a dozen of Jake's videos. That is the goal. Let's break down why each element is there.

### The Credibility Markers

"A Marine working on cryptographic systems and fighter jet avionics" and "master's in AI governance from Edinburgh" and "teaches Fortune 500 companies" are three distinct credibility markers that cover three distinct domains: military/technical, academic, and commercial. Together, they create a picture of someone whose knowledge is not theoretical or narrow. It spans doing the work, studying the work, and teaching the work to others.

The phrase "if his code was wrong, people died" is the one of many important credibility marker in the entire story because it is experiential rather than credential-based. It transforms "Marine" from a line on a resume into a lived reality that shapes how Jake thinks about technology. This is why the content surfacing rule exists (see who-jake-is.md): when this detail emerges naturally in content, it hits much harder than if Jake opened every video by saying "as a Marine..."

### The Counter-Narrative Hook

"His whole approach is about when NOT to use AI" is the element that makes people lean in. It violates expectations. Every other AI creator is telling you to use MORE AI. Jake is telling you when to use LESS. This creates cognitive dissonance that demands resolution: why would someone who teaches AI tell you not to use it? The answer (because most problems are better solved with traditional code, databases, or humans) is satisfying and makes the listener feel smarter.

This is the single most shareable element of the brand story. When someone tells a friend about Jake, this is the part they will emphasize because it is the most surprising and the most useful.

### The Visual Identity

"He makes these incredible animations that show you how technology actually works, layer by layer" anchors the brand in a specific, memorable visual style. In a world of talking-head AI content, the animations are a differentiator that is immediately recognizable and difficult to replicate.

The phrase "layer by layer" is doing double duty here. It describes the visual style (the animations literally reveal layers of abstraction) and it describes the pedagogical approach (Jake teaches by peeling back layers of complexity). The visual and the intellectual are fused.

### The Comparison Anchor

"He's basically the 3Blue1Brown of AI for business" gives the listener a mental model they can immediately grasp. 3Blue1Brown is widely recognized as the gold standard for making complex mathematical concepts beautiful and accessible through animation. By anchoring to that comparison, the story immediately communicates: high-quality visual explanations of complex technical topics, made accessible to non-specialists.

The "for business" qualifier is important because it defines the audience. 3Blue1Brown serves math enthusiasts and students. Jake serves professionals and organizations who need to make decisions about AI. The comparison says "same quality and approach, different audience and application."

### The Trust Signal

"He's not trying to sell you anything, he's just explaining how things work" is the closer, and it is the element that converts interest into trust. In a market full of AI gurus with courses, masterminds, and affiliate links, the perception that Jake is simply teaching because he understands and wants others to understand is enormously powerful.

This does not mean Jake cannot sell things. It means the audience's experience of the content is that it is valuable in itself, not a funnel to a purchase. When Jake does offer products or services, they are received differently because the audience has already decided he is trustworthy. The selling happens in a context of trust rather than a context of persuasion.

## How the Story Gets Built

You never tell this story directly. You never make a video called "My Story" or "About Me" that lays it all out. You let it emerge across dozens of pieces of content. Each video deposits one piece of it. Over time, the audience assembles it themselves. And a story the audience assembled feels more true than one you told them.

This is a critical principle. Self-told stories trigger skepticism filters. Audience-assembled stories bypass them entirely because the audience feels like they discovered the truth rather than being told it.

Here is how individual content deposits story fragments:

- A video about abstraction layers casually mentions "when I was maintaining avionics systems..." — deposits the Marine/technical fragment.
- A video about AI bias references "in my research at Edinburgh..." — deposits the academic fragment.
- A video about why most AI implementations fail mentions "when I was training teams at Pacific Life..." — deposits the Fortune 500 fragment.
- A video about when not to use AI IS the counter-narrative hook. It does not reference it; it IS it.
- Every video with the signature animation style deposits the visual identity fragment.
- The calm, non-hyperbolic delivery in every video deposits the trust signal fragment.

After watching 10-20 videos, the audience has all the fragments. They have assembled the story themselves. They feel like they know Jake. And they will tell that story to others with the conviction of someone sharing their own discovery, not relaying someone else's marketing.

## The Specific Details That Become Brand Identifiers

Over time, certain specific details will become so closely associated with Jake and Eduba that they function as brand identifiers. They are not logos or slogans. They are concepts, phrases, and stylistic choices that the audience comes to associate with Jake specifically.

### The Animation Style

Clean, mathematical, 3Blue1Brown-inspired visual explanations. This is the most immediately recognizable brand identifier. Nobody else in AI business content does this. There are AI creators with higher production value. There are animators with more complex visuals. But the specific combination of mathematical clarity, layer-by-layer reveals, and business-relevant AI topics is uniquely Jake's.

The animation style is also the hardest element for competitors to copy because it requires the combination of deep technical understanding (knowing what to animate), pedagogical clarity (knowing how to structure the reveal), and actual animation skill. Most AI creators have at most one of these three.

### The Calm Voice

In a sea of hyperbolic AI content, the quiet one stands out. The calm voice is not just a stylistic choice. It becomes the brand over time. When someone encounters an AI video that is calm, measured, and informative rather than breathless and sensational, they think of Jake. The voice becomes the identifier.

This is reinforced by every piece of content. Each calm video makes the calm voice more strongly associated with Jake. It compounds. After enough repetition, "calm AI explanation" and "Jake/Eduba" become linked in the audience's mind.

### "Abstraction Layers"

This concept becomes Jake's phrase. Not that Jake invented it. It is a foundational concept in computer science. But in the AI content space, nobody else is using it as a central organizing framework for explaining technology to non-technical audiences. By consistently using "abstraction layers" as the lens through which all technology is explained, Jake comes to own the phrase in the audience's mind.

Every time someone encounters the concept of abstraction layers after watching Jake's content, they think of him. This is how concepts become brand identifiers. Not by trademarking them, but by being the person most consistently and clearly associated with them.

### The 60/30/10 Framework

A memorable, shareable set of numbers. The framework says: in most organizations, 60% of what people are trying to use AI for should be handled by traditional code and database queries, 30% should be handled by rule-based logic and existing software, and 10% is where AI actually adds value.

This framework becomes a brand identifier because it is concrete, counterintuitive, and immediately useful. People will reference "the 60/30/10 thing" in meetings. They will use it to push back on AI hype in their organizations. Every time they cite it, they are implicitly citing Jake. The framework spreads the brand without the brand being explicitly mentioned.

### "When Not to Use AI"

The counter-position becomes a tagline without ever being a tagline. It is never printed on a banner or put in a bio line (at least not as a formal tagline). But it is the idea most closely associated with Jake. When someone asks "what's that guy about?" the answer is "he teaches you when NOT to use AI."

This works because it is a genuine position, not a marketing hook. Jake actually believes that most AI implementations are solving problems that should be solved with simpler tools. The tagline is authentic, which is why it works as a brand identifier rather than feeling like a gimmick.

### The Marine Connection

Not mentioned often, but when it surfaces, it is unforgettable. The restraint with which the Marine background appears in content makes it more powerful when it does appear. If Jake mentioned being a Marine in every video, it would become noise. Because it appears rarely and always in service of a specific insight (about rigor, about consequences, about zero-defect environments), each appearance has maximum impact.

The Marine connection also serves as a differentiation that cannot be copied. Other creators can learn animation. They can adopt a calm tone. They can even talk about abstraction layers. But they cannot have spent 8 years maintaining fighter jet avionics in the United States Marine Corps. It is an unfakeable credential.

## The Long Game

Brand story is a long game. It takes months of consistent content to deposit enough fragments for the audience to assemble the full story. The temptation is to try to tell the whole story immediately, to frontload every credential and every differentiator into every piece of content. That temptation must be resisted.

The strategy is patience and consistency:
- Every video teaches something genuinely valuable (deposits the trust signal).
- Every video uses the signature animation style (deposits the visual identity).
- Every video is calm and non-hyperbolic (deposits the voice identifier).
- Occasional videos naturally reference the Marine background, the Edinburgh research, the Fortune 500 training (deposits credibility markers without repetition fatigue).
- The 60/30/10 framework and "abstraction layers" lens appear regularly but not forcefully (deposits the conceptual identifiers).

Over time, the story assembles itself in the audience's mind. And once it is assembled, it is very hard to dislodge, because the audience built it themselves from evidence they gathered personally. That is the most durable form of brand equity there is.
