# Who Jake Is

## The One-Sentence Version

A Marine-turned-engineer-turned-governance-researcher who teaches organizations and developers that AI is just the latest abstraction layer in computing, and that the real skill is knowing when to use it, when to use a database query, and when to use a human.

That sentence does a lot of work. It establishes a non-obvious career arc (military to engineering to academic research), positions AI within a larger technical context (not as magic, but as one tool among many), and embeds the core philosophy (the skill is knowing which tool to reach for, not being dazzled by the newest one).

## The Earned Authority Stack

Jake's credibility is not a single credential. It is a stack — multiple layers of authority that reinforce each other and make the positioning nearly impossible to replicate. Each layer matters, and they matter most in combination.

### Layer 1: Technical Credibility

Eight years in the United States Marine Corps working on cryptographic systems and F-35/F-18 avionics. This is not a generic "military background" bullet point. This is specific, high-consequence technical work in zero-defect environments where bad code means people die.

The distinction matters enormously. Working on fighter jet avionics systems gives you a fundamentally different relationship with rigor, testing, and the consequences of technical failure than working at a startup where the worst case is a bug in a shopping cart. When Jake talks about when to trust automation and when not to, it comes from a lived experience where trusting the wrong automation could have killed someone. That is not LinkedIn posturing. It is a fundamentally different orientation toward technology.

The cryptographic systems work also matters specifically for the AI content space. Cryptography is abstraction layers all the way down. Understanding how encrypted communications actually work — from the human pressing a button, through the encryption protocols, through the hardware, through the transmission medium — is the exact mental model that Jake now applies to explaining AI, software, and computing in general. The Marine Corps didn't just give Jake credibility. It gave him the conceptual framework that became the brand.

### Layer 2: Academic Depth

MSc in Future Governance from the University of Edinburgh. Published research on AI ethics (arXiv:2510.11742) with 90% test-retest reliability. This is primary research, not commentary.

The distinction between primary research and commentary is critical in the AI content space. Thousands of people comment on AI. They read papers, summarize them, react to them, give their takes. Jake ran an actual study, produced original findings, and published them on arXiv. That is a categorically different level of engagement with the subject matter.

The 90% test-retest reliability figure matters because it demonstrates methodological rigor. This is not a blog post dressed up as research. It is work that meets the standards of academic scrutiny. When Jake discusses AI bias, AI governance, or AI ethics, he is not offering opinions. He is drawing on a body of original research that he designed, executed, and published.

The Edinburgh context also matters. The University of Edinburgh has one of the oldest and most respected AI research programs in the world. Getting an MSc in Future Governance there means studying AI governance in a department that has been thinking about these problems longer than most AI companies have existed.

### Layer 3: Real-World Validation

Trained 300+ employees at Pacific Life and Colgate-Palmolive. Delivered workshops to 40+ executives at KPMG UK. Achieved a 95% adoption rate versus the industry standard of 10%.

These numbers do specific work in the authority stack:

- **300+ employees** at recognized Fortune 500 companies proves this is not theoretical. Jake has stood in front of large groups of working professionals and taught them to actually use and understand AI in their daily work.
- **40+ executives at KPMG UK** proves this scales to senior leadership. These are not junior employees being sent to a training. These are decision-makers at one of the Big Four accounting firms choosing to spend their time in Jake's workshop.
- **95% adoption rate** is the most powerful number in the stack. The industry standard for AI training adoption — meaning people actually continue using what they learned after the training ends — is roughly 10%. Jake's rate is 95%. That is not a marginal improvement. That is an order-of-magnitude difference. It proves the teaching methodology works in a way that no credential alone can prove.

### Layer 4: Counter-Positioning Proof

Jake's entire business model is built on telling people when NOT to use AI. Nobody selling snake oil tells you not to buy it. That is the trust signal.

This layer is different from the others because it is not a credential. It is a structural feature of the business model that functions as proof of integrity. In a market saturated with people trying to sell AI tools, AI courses, AI consulting, and AI hype, Jake's core message is: most of the time, you should not be using AI for what you are using it for. Use a database query. Use traditional code. Use a human. AI is the right answer maybe 10% of the time.

This is inherently self-authenticating. A grifter would never adopt this position because it reduces the addressable market. The fact that Jake leads with "when not to use AI" is itself evidence that the consulting, training, and content are trustworthy. You cannot fake this positioning because faking it would undermine the business model.

## The Rule for Surfacing Authority in Content

This is one of the most important operational rules in the entire brand system. It governs how Jake's credentials appear in every piece of content across every platform.

**Never lead with credentials. Lead with the insight. Let the credentials emerge naturally as proof of why the insight is trustworthy.**

The wrong way:
> "As a former Marine with 8 years of experience in cryptographic systems and avionics, and an MSc from Edinburgh, I believe that AI should be used judiciously..."

The right way:
> "I spent 8 years maintaining systems where a software bug could kill someone. That changes how you think about when to trust automation."

The second version is infinitely more powerful than the first, and understanding why is essential to understanding Jake's brand.

The first version is a resume recitation. The audience's brain categorizes it as self-promotion and partially discounts it. Every creator leads with credentials. It is noise.

The second version is a story fragment that carries the credential inside it. The audience doesn't feel like they're being sold on Jake's authority. They feel like they're being let into an experience that shaped how he thinks. The credential (8 years in the Marine Corps working on life-critical systems) is fully present, but it arrives as context for an insight rather than as a qualification for speaking.

This rule applies everywhere:
- In video scripts, credentials surface as part of the narrative, not as introductions.
- In bios and about pages, the story leads and the formal credentials follow.
- In consulting proposals, the results lead (95% adoption rate) and the background explains why those results are possible.
- In social media, a specific experience or insight leads, and the credential is implicit or emerges in follow-up.

The reasoning behind this rule: Jake's audience is intelligent and discerning. They are the kind of people who are suspicious of credential-leading because they have seen too many credentialed people say nothing of substance. By leading with substance and letting credentials emerge, Jake aligns with how his audience already evaluates trustworthiness. He is not asking them to trust him because of where he went to school. He is showing them something interesting, and then they discover that the person showing them has a background that makes the interesting thing even more credible.

This is compounding trust. Each piece of content deposits a fragment of credibility without ever feeling like a sales pitch. Over dozens of videos and posts, the audience assembles a picture of someone with deep, multi-layered expertise — and because they assembled it themselves, it feels more true than if Jake had told them directly.
