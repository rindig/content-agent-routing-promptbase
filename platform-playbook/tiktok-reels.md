# TikTok & Instagram Reels Strategy

## Platform Purpose

TikTok and Instagram Reels are **discovery engines**. They are the primary mechanism for maximum reach and top-of-funnel growth. These platforms exist in the content strategy to answer one question: how do we get Jake's content in front of people who have never heard of him?

The algorithm on both platforms is designed to surface content to non-followers. Unlike YouTube (where subscribers and search drive views) or LinkedIn (where your network determines reach), TikTok and Reels actively push content to strangers based on engagement signals. This makes them the ideal top-of-funnel channel.

Every video posted here should be evaluated primarily on one criterion: **does this make a stranger want to see more from this person?**

---

## What to Post

**Primarily animated explainer shorts, 30-60 seconds.**

These platforms are visual-first environments. The animated explainer format is the strongest content type for this context because:

1. **Animation carries understanding.** Complex technical concepts that would require paragraphs of text or minutes of talking-head explanation can be conveyed in seconds through well-designed animation. The visual IS the explanation, not a supplement to it.

2. **Visual-first content stops the scroll.** On TikTok and Reels, the user is scrolling through a feed of talking heads, lip syncs, and lifestyle content. An animated technical explainer is visually distinct. It stops the thumb because it looks different from everything around it.

3. **The strongest hooks land hardest here.** The hook styles that work best for Eduba content (Quiet Reveals, Number Anchors, Counter-Positions) are all enhanced by animation. A Quiet Reveal paired with a visual that slowly builds is more compelling than the same hook delivered as a talking head.

Content posted here should be the sharpest, most hook-optimized versions of each video. If a script works well but the hook is not razor-sharp, it may perform on YouTube but should not lead on TikTok/Reels where the first moment is disproportionately important.

---

## Posting Frequency

**5-7 posts per week.**

The current production pipeline (animated explainer shorts with voiceover) supports this volume. Consistency matters more than perfection on these platforms -- the algorithm rewards accounts that post regularly and penalizes gaps. Five per week is the floor; seven is the target when production is running smoothly.

This frequency also generates the data volume needed to understand what works. With 5-7 posts per week, patterns in hook performance, topic resonance, and audience retention become visible within weeks rather than months.

## Optimization Notes

### The First 1-2 Seconds Are Everything

This is not an exaggeration or a general principle. On TikTok and Reels, the algorithm evaluates content primarily on early retention -- the percentage of viewers who are still watching after the first 1-2 seconds. A video with a strong first second and mediocre middle will outperform a video with a weak first second and brilliant middle, because the second video will never get the chance to show its brilliance to a large audience.

Practical implications:
- The hook must land in the opening frame. Not the first 5 seconds. The first 1-2 seconds.
- Do not open with a logo, an intro, a greeting, or any "throat clearing." Open with the hook itself.
- The first visual frame should be compelling on its own. If someone paused the video at the 0.5-second mark, the single frozen frame should be interesting enough to make them unpause.

### Caption the Hook as On-Screen Text

A significant percentage of TikTok and Reels viewers scroll with sound off, at least initially. They decide whether to turn sound on based on what they see in the first moment.

The first line of the hook should appear as on-screen text so that silent scrollers get the hook visually. This is not a subtitle -- it is a deliberate design choice. The text should be:
- Large enough to read on a phone screen
- Positioned where the eye naturally goes (upper third or center)
- Styled consistently across videos so returning viewers recognize the format

This on-screen hook text serves a dual purpose: it catches silent scrollers, and it reinforces the audio hook for viewers who do have sound on. Two channels delivering the same hook simultaneously increases retention.

### Trending Sounds: Use Sparingly and Intentionally

Trending sounds can boost discoverability, but they must be used carefully for Eduba's content type:

- **Voiceover is the primary audio.** Jake's voice explaining concepts is the core of the content. A trending sound should never replace or compete with the voiceover.
- **Use trending sounds only when they genuinely enhance.** A trending background track that adds energy without distracting from the voiceover is fine. A trending sound that requires lip-syncing or changes the tone of the content is not aligned with the brand.
- **Do not chase trends that compromise the content.** A video that uses a trending sound but delivers a weaker explanation is a worse video, even if the sound temporarily boosts reach. The brand is built on substance, and every video reinforces or undermines that brand.

### Hashtag Strategy

Use a mix of broad and niche hashtags to balance discovery with targeting:

**Broad hashtags** (high volume, wide reach):
- #coding
- #ai
- #tech
- #programming
- #learntocode
- #technology

**Niche hashtags** (lower volume, more targeted audience):
- #softwarearchitecture
- #aitools
- #promptengineering
- #systemdesign
- #techexplained
- #codingconcepts

The broad hashtags put content into large discovery pools where the algorithm can find an audience. The niche hashtags signal to the algorithm what kind of content this is, helping it find the right viewers within those large pools.

Do not use more than 5-8 hashtags total. Overloading hashtags can signal low-quality content to the algorithm and visually clutters the caption.

### Reply to Comments with Follow-Up Video Shorts

When a comment on a video asks an interesting question, create a short follow-up video that answers it. This serves two strategic purposes simultaneously:

1. **Feeds the algorithm.** Reply videos create engagement loops. The original commenter gets notified, comes back, watches the reply, and likely engages again. The algorithm sees this interaction chain and boosts both videos.

2. **Generates content ideas.** Comments reveal what the audience is curious about after watching a video. These are not hypothetical content ideas -- they are validated interests from real viewers. A comment asking "but what about edge cases in X?" is a content idea that has already been market-tested.

This tactic also builds community. Viewers who see their comments turned into videos feel seen and valued. They become repeat engagers, which further feeds the algorithm. It creates a virtuous cycle: good content generates interesting comments, interesting comments generate more content, more content generates more comments.

## Platform-Specific Format Notes

### TikTok
- Vertical video: 9:16 aspect ratio, 1080x1920 resolution
- Optimal length: 30-60 seconds (long enough to deliver value, short enough for high completion rates)
- Bio link: Use link-in-bio tool to route to relevant offers based on funnel position
- Profile: Clean, professional, makes it immediately clear what the account is about

### Instagram Reels
- Same 9:16 format as TikTok
- Reels can be slightly longer (up to 90 seconds) but 30-60 seconds remains the sweet spot
- Instagram rewards content that also generates saves and shares, not just views. Content that viewers want to reference later (which technical explainers naturally are) tends to perform well.
- Cross-post from TikTok but remove the TikTok watermark (Instagram deprioritizes content with competitor watermarks). Re-export the original file rather than downloading from TikTok.
- Use Instagram Stories to tease upcoming Reels and drive followers to the content
