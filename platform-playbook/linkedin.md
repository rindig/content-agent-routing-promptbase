# LinkedIn Strategy

## Platform Purpose

LinkedIn serves as the **professional credibility engine and decision-maker pipeline.** This is not a reach platform -- it is a conversion platform. Specifically, it is where Tier 3 audience members (decision-makers, CTOs, VPs of Engineering, technical leadership) encounter Jake's thinking in a professional context and begin the path toward consulting engagements.

While TikTok and Reels bring people into the world and YouTube compounds the content library, LinkedIn is where business happens. The audience here is not scrolling for entertainment -- they are scanning for insight that helps them make better decisions in their organizations. The content must meet them where they are.

**LinkedIn is where Tier 3 (decision-maker) conversions happen most. Treat it accordingly.** Every post should be written with the awareness that the reader may be someone who could hire Jake for a five-figure consulting engagement. This does not mean every post should sell -- it means every post should demonstrate the kind of thinking that makes someone want to hire the person who wrote it.

---

## What to Post

**Written posts (150-300 words) with optional video clips.**

LinkedIn is a text-first platform. Unlike TikTok/Reels (video-first) or YouTube (video + search), LinkedIn's primary content format is the written post. Video can supplement and boost reach, but the writing carries the substance.

The tone on LinkedIn should be **more explicitly professional and business-oriented** than on other platforms. The same core ideas (abstraction layers, methods over tools, honest takes on AI) are expressed here, but through a business lens:

- On TikTok: "Here's what actually happens when AI processes your prompt" (curiosity-driven)
- On LinkedIn: "Most organizations are misunderstanding how LLMs process context, and it's costing them in production reliability" (business-impact-driven)

The underlying insight is the same. The framing shifts to match the audience's context and concerns.

### Content Types for LinkedIn

- **Concise insight posts (150-300 words):** A single clear idea, well-articulated, with a business or professional angle. These are the bread and butter.
- **Framework explanations:** The 60/30/10 rule, abstraction layer thinking, build-vs-buy frameworks -- these land exceptionally well on LinkedIn because the audience is actively making these decisions.
- **Industry analysis:** Commentary on AI trends, vendor announcements, or industry shifts. More opinionated and business-focused than what would go on TikTok.
- **Case studies (anonymized):** "A client came to us with X challenge. Here's how we thought about it." These naturally demonstrate consulting value without explicitly selling.
- **Video clips:** Short clips from the animated explainer library, uploaded natively to LinkedIn (not linked from YouTube or TikTok). These supplement the written content and boost algorithmic reach.

---

## Posting Frequency

**3-4 posts per week.**

LinkedIn does not require the volume that TikTok does. The algorithm rewards engagement depth (comments, shares, time spent reading) more than posting frequency. A well-crafted post that generates thoughtful comments will outperform three mediocre posts.

The 3-4 per week target allows for:
- 2-3 written insight posts
- 1 video clip with accompanying written context
- Optional: 1 longer-form post (industry analysis or framework explanation) when the topic warrants it

## Optimization Notes

### The First 2-3 Lines Are the Hook

LinkedIn shows only the first 2-3 lines of a post before truncating with a "...see more" link. This means the first 2-3 lines function identically to a video hook -- they determine whether anyone reads the rest.

**The hook goes here. Not a greeting. Not context-setting. The hook.**

Bad opening:
> "I've been thinking a lot about AI adoption lately, and I wanted to share some thoughts on what I've been seeing in the industry..."

This tells the reader nothing. It is throat-clearing. No one clicks "see more" on this.

Good opening:
> "Most companies are spending 6 figures on AI tools they'll abandon within 18 months. The problem isn't the tools."

This creates immediate tension. The reader knows the topic (AI tool adoption), has a concrete number (6 figures, 18 months), and faces an open question (if not the tools, then what?). They click "see more."

**Principles for LinkedIn hooks:**
- State a specific, surprising claim in the first line
- Include a concrete number or timeframe when possible
- Create an open loop that the "see more" click resolves
- Write as if the first 2 lines are the only lines most people will see (because they are)

### Native Video Outperforms Links

LinkedIn's algorithm heavily favors native content over external links. A video uploaded directly to LinkedIn will receive dramatically more reach than a link to the same video on YouTube.

**Upload clips directly.** Do not post YouTube links or TikTok links. Download the original video file and upload it natively to LinkedIn. The momentary inconvenience is worth the reach difference, which can be 5-10x.

When posting video, include written context above the video. LinkedIn video posts with substantial written context outperform posts that are just a video with no text. The written content gives the algorithm text to index and gives viewers who cannot watch video (in meetings, on mute) a reason to engage.

### Strategic Tagging

Tag relevant companies and people when there is **genuine relevance** -- not as a reach hack.

Good tagging:
- Tagging a company when discussing their publicly released AI tool or announcement
- Tagging a person whose published work or public talk you are responding to
- Tagging a co-panelist or collaborator on shared work

Bad tagging:
- Tagging companies just because they are in the AI space
- Tagging influencers hoping they will reshare
- Tagging 10+ people on a post that is not specifically about them

Spam-tagging damages credibility on LinkedIn faster than on any other platform. The audience here is sophisticated and recognizes engagement-farming tactics immediately. One well-placed, genuinely relevant tag is worth more than ten spray-and-pray tags.

### Comment on Other People's Posts

This is one of the highest-ROI activities on LinkedIn and is unique to this platform. On TikTok, commenting on other people's videos does little for your own visibility. On LinkedIn, a substantive comment on a popular post can generate significant profile visits and followers.

**Write substantive takes, not generic agreement.** "Great post!" does nothing. A 3-4 sentence comment that adds a new angle, provides a counterpoint, or shares a relevant experience positions Jake as a thoughtful voice in the conversation.

Target posts from:
- Other AI/tech commentators with large audiences
- Industry leaders discussing AI strategy or adoption
- News posts about major AI developments or announcements

The comment should demonstrate the same clear thinking and grounded perspective that the main content does. Every comment is a micro-piece of content that reaches an audience Jake might not otherwise reach.

### Longer-Form Insights for LinkedIn

Some content is too business-focused for TikTok but perfect for LinkedIn. This includes:
- **Case studies** with enough detail to be useful (anonymized but specific)
- **Framework explanations** that require more than 60 seconds to properly articulate
- **Industry analysis** that assumes business context and decision-making authority
- **Consulting-adjacent insights** that naturally demonstrate the value of working with Jake

These longer-form pieces (500-800 words) should be posted occasionally (1-2 per month) as LinkedIn articles or extended posts. They serve as credibility anchors -- pieces that a potential consulting client can read and think "this person understands my problems."

## LinkedIn and the Funnel

LinkedIn spans the funnel differently than other platforms:

- **Top of funnel on LinkedIn** looks like sharp insight posts that get shared widely. Someone sees Jake's comment or a reshared post and visits the profile for the first time.
- **Middle of funnel on LinkedIn** looks like framework posts and industry analysis. The reader follows Jake, sees regular posts, and develops a sense of "this person thinks clearly about the problems I'm dealing with."
- **Bottom of funnel on LinkedIn** looks like case studies and consulting-adjacent content. The reader has been following for weeks or months, sees a case study that mirrors their own challenges, and reaches out via DM or the website.

The key difference from other platforms: **LinkedIn's bottom of funnel is the most direct path to high-value consulting conversions.** A CTO who has been reading Jake's LinkedIn posts for three months and then sees a case study about solving the exact problem their organization faces is the highest-intent lead in the entire content system.

This is why LinkedIn deserves dedicated attention despite having a smaller audience than TikTok or YouTube. The per-follower value on LinkedIn is orders of magnitude higher for the consulting business model.

## LinkedIn-Specific Format Notes

- **Post length:** 150-300 words for standard posts. 500-800 words for occasional deep dives.
- **Line breaks:** Use single-line paragraphs with line breaks between them. Dense paragraphs get skipped on LinkedIn. White space is a readability tool.
- **Emojis:** Use sparingly or not at all. The Eduba brand is grounded and professional. Bullet-point emojis are acceptable if they aid readability, but emoji-heavy posts signal a different kind of creator than what Jake is.
- **Hashtags:** 3-5 relevant hashtags at the bottom of the post. LinkedIn hashtags function more like topic tags than discovery mechanisms. Use: #AI #ArtificialIntelligence #SoftwareArchitecture #TechStrategy #AIAdoption as a base set.
- **Profile optimization:** The LinkedIn profile itself should be treated as a landing page. Headline should communicate what Jake does and for whom. About section should be a concise version of the Eduba positioning. Featured section should showcase the strongest content pieces.
