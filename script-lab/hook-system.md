# The Hook System

Six hook types for all Eduba content. Every script starts with a hook. Read this before writing any script.

---

## Core Principle: Your Calm IS the Hook

Quiet confidence in a feed full of hype. Hooks sound like a smart, calm person who knows something you don't. Declarative, not exclamatory. Understated authority — the facts speak, not the volume.

**What calm sounds like:**
- "Here's what's actually happening" not "You won't BELIEVE what's happening"
- Measured pacing — let the ideas breathe
- Conversational, not academic (contractions are fine)

For full voice rules, see `../brand-vault/voice-and-tone.md` → "Voice Rules for Content" section.

## Hook Type 1: "The Quiet Reveal"

**Structure:** [Thing you think is simple] + [It's actually not] + [Here's why that matters]

Best for P1 (Layers Beneath), works across all pillars when there's a gap between perception and reality.

### Examples

- "When you ask ChatGPT a question, you probably think it's searching for the answer. It's not. It's predicting the next word. And that distinction changes everything about how you should use it."

- "Most people think AI is the most complex part of a modern app. It's usually less than 10% of the code. The other 90% is stuff we solved decades ago."

- "You've probably heard that AI learns like a human brain. It doesn't. And once you understand how it actually learns, you'll know exactly when to trust it and when not to."

- "Every time you run an AI query, you're spending roughly 10x what a database query costs. For most of what businesses use AI for, the database query gives you the same answer."

- "There's a reason your AI-generated code works for small projects but falls apart at scale. It's the same reason a GPS can navigate a city but can't design one."

### Pattern Notes
- The first sentence establishes the assumption. Use "you probably think" or "most people think" to make the viewer self-identify.
- The second sentence is the pivot. Keep it short and direct. "It's not." "It doesn't." "It's usually less than..."
- The third sentence is the promise. It tells the viewer WHY the distinction matters to them personally. Without this, the reveal is just a fun fact. With it, the reveal is useful knowledge.

## Hook Type 2: "The Number Anchor"

**Structure:** [Specific surprising number] + [What it means]

Best for P2 (Right Tool) and P5 (Honest Take) — data and measurement are natural parts of the argument.

### Examples

- "That one line of Python triggers roughly 12,000 lines of code across 7 different layers."

- "I trained 300 employees at Fortune 500 companies on AI. The first thing I taught every single one of them was when NOT to use it."

- "60% of what companies are calling 'AI projects' should be database queries. 30% should be traditional code. Only 10% actually needs AI."

- "The average AI implementation has a 10% adoption rate. Ours hit 95%. The difference isn't the AI. It's what you build around it."

- "A single GPT-4 query costs roughly 100x what the same question would cost as a SQL query. For most business questions, they give you the same answer."

### Pattern Notes
- Lead with the number. Don't bury it. The number IS the hook.
- Use "roughly" or "about" when the number is approximate. Precision without accuracy is dishonest. Jake's brand is built on honesty.
- The second part must connect the number to a lesson or insight. A number alone is trivia. A number with meaning is a hook.
- Avoid round numbers when possible. "12,000" is more believable than "10,000" because it implies actual measurement rather than estimation.

## Hook Type 3: "The Reframe"

**Structure:** [Common way of thinking about X] + [Here's a more useful way to think about it]

Best for P3 (Methods Not Tools) — teaching thinking frameworks, not tool-specific knowledge. Gives the viewer a new mental model, not just new information.

### Examples

- "Everyone talks about AI like it's magic. But it's actually a compiler. It takes human language as input and produces structured output. And once you see it that way, everything about how to use it becomes obvious."

- "People keep asking which AI tool is best. That's like asking which wrench is best. The answer is: it depends on the bolt. Let me show you how to look at the bolt."

- "Coding isn't really about writing code. It's about decomposing problems into layers that machines can execute. AI just changed which layer the human works at."

- "The question isn't 'will AI replace my job.' The question is 'which parts of my job are actually AI-shaped problems and which parts aren't.' Most people are surprised by the answer."

- "Stop thinking of AI as a brain and start thinking of it as a very expensive calculator that's really good at one specific type of math."

### Pattern Notes
- State the old mental model first. Use "everyone talks about" or "people keep asking" to establish it as common thinking.
- Introduce the new model with a concrete analogy. "It's actually a compiler." "That's like asking which wrench is best." Analogies are how mental models transfer.
- End with the implication. "Once you see it that way..." or "Let me show you..." The viewer needs to know that the new model will be useful to them, not just intellectually interesting.
- The best Reframes are ones the viewer will repeat to other people. If it is quotable, it is working.

## Hook Type 4: "The Counter-Position"

**Structure:** [What everyone in the space is saying] + [Why that's incomplete or wrong] + [What's actually true]

Best for P5 (Honest Take). Not contrarianism — every counter-position must be grounded in real experience, data, or reasoning. Calm disagreement, not angry takedowns.

### Examples

- "Every AI influencer is telling you to automate everything. I'm going to tell you the opposite. Here's when automation actually makes things worse."

- "The AI industry wants you to believe you need AI for everything. I've spent years helping Fortune 500 companies figure out that most of what they need is a well-designed spreadsheet."

- "Everyone's talking about prompt engineering like it's a new discipline. It's not. It's just communication skills with a fancy name. Let me show you what actually matters."

- "You're being told to 'just vibe code' and AI will handle the rest. Here's what happens to those projects six months later."

- "The most valuable AI skill isn't knowing how to use AI. It's knowing when not to. And almost nobody is teaching that."

### Pattern Notes
- Name the prevailing narrative explicitly. "Every AI influencer is telling you" or "You're being told to..." The viewer needs to recognize the thing you are pushing back against.
- The disagreement should feel measured, not angry. "I'm going to tell you the opposite" is calm. "Everyone is LYING to you" is not Jake's voice.
- Always offer the better alternative. Counter-positioning without a constructive replacement is just criticism. Jake doesn't tear things down -- he reveals what is actually true.
- This hook type naturally attracts a certain audience: people who are skeptical of hype and looking for a trusted, grounded voice. Those are exactly Jake's target viewers.

## Hook Type 5: "The Earned Authority Super Hook"

**Structure:** [Credential or experience compressed into the hook itself] + [What it taught you]

**Use sparingly — 1 in 10 videos max.** The credential is integrated into the hook, not preamble. Only use when the experience IS the story and directly creates the insight.

### Examples

- "I spent 8 years maintaining cryptographic systems for the Marine Corps. The kind where a software bug doesn't mean a bad user experience, it means someone doesn't come home. That changes how you think about trusting AI."

- "I published peer-reviewed research on how AI models actually behave when you test them for bias. 90% of what the industry says about AI ethics is marketing. Here's what the data actually shows."

- "I've trained executives at four Fortune 500 companies on AI. The single biggest misconception every single one had was the same, and it's probably the one you have too."

- "My thesis measured AI bias with 90% reliability. Most companies measuring AI bias are using methods with the statistical rigor of a coin flip. Here's the difference."

- "I went from maintaining fighter jet avionics to teaching organizations about AI. The lesson that transferred perfectly was this: the system that works is the one the human actually understands."

### Pattern Notes
- The credential must be specific. "I have experience in tech" is worthless. "I spent 8 years maintaining cryptographic systems for the Marine Corps" is vivid and verifiable.
- The transition from credential to insight must feel inevitable. The reader should feel that OF COURSE someone with that background would see things this way.
- Never use this hook type just to impress. Use it when the experience genuinely created a unique insight that could not have come from anywhere else.
- The insight at the end should still be the star. The credential is the setup. The insight is the payoff.

## Hook Type 6: "The Question Spiral"

**Structure:** [Interesting question] that leads to [deeper question] that leads to [the real insight]

Best for P1 (Layers Beneath) — each question triggers a new visual layer in animation. Longer than other hooks by design (budget 15-20 seconds in a 60-90s video).

### Examples

- "Why does ChatGPT sometimes confidently say things that are completely wrong? To understand that, you first need to understand what a probability distribution is. And to understand that, you need to see what's actually happening inside the model. Let's go layer by layer."

- "Why do most AI projects fail? It's not the AI. It's not the data. It's that nobody asked the right question first. What's the right question? It starts with understanding what kind of problem you actually have."

- "What would happen if you removed AI from most 'AI-powered' products? Surprisingly, about 60% of them would work just fine. Why? Because the AI was solving a problem that didn't need AI. Let me show you how to tell the difference."

### Pattern Notes
- Start with a question the viewer has probably wondered about.
- Each subsequent question should go one level deeper. Zooming in, not wandering sideways.
- End with an invitation: "Let's go layer by layer" or "Let me show you."

## Choosing the Right Hook Type

There is no formula, but here are guidelines:

| If the content is about... | Consider... |
|---|---|
| How something actually works under the hood | Type 1 (Quiet Reveal) or Type 6 (Question Spiral) |
| A surprising data point or measurement | Type 2 (Number Anchor) |
| A new way to think about a familiar concept | Type 3 (Reframe) |
| Pushing back against industry hype or consensus | Type 4 (Counter-Position) |
| A lesson that came from specific lived experience | Type 5 (Earned Authority) -- use sparingly |
| A complex topic that requires building understanding in layers | Type 6 (Question Spiral) |

**Combining hook types:** Some of the strongest hooks combine elements from multiple types. A Number Anchor inside a Counter-Position ("Everyone says AI is the future. 60% of AI projects should be database queries."). A Quiet Reveal that spirals into questions. Combinations work as long as the hook remains clear, calm, and under control. If it starts to feel busy or forced, simplify.

## Anti-Patterns

- **Never hype.** No "INSANE," "GAME-CHANGER," "You NEED to see this." Banned words.
- **Never beg.** No "Please watch till the end."
- **Never mislead.** Curiosity gaps fine. Bait-and-switch not.
- **Never lead with credentials by default.** Type 5 only, sparingly.
- **Never sound generic.** If any AI creator could have written it, rewrite it.
- **Never rush.** A 10-second hook that lands > a 3-second hook that doesn't register.
